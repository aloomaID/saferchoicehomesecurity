What should I do if my home security system triggers a false alarm?

False alarms can be a frustrating issue with home security systems, but knowing how to handle them can help minimize stress and maintain your system’s reliability. At Safer Choice Home Security, we guide homeowners in Laie Hawaii through the necessary steps to manage false alarms effectively.
1. Stay Calm and Assess: The first step is to remain calm. False alarms can occur due to a variety of reasons, such as a misaligned sensor or an accidental trigger. Quickly assess the situation to determine whether the alarm is indeed false or if there might be a real security concern.
2. Verify the Source: Check the security system’s control panel or app to identify which sensor or camera triggered the alarm. This will help you pinpoint the problem and address it appropriately. Safer Choice Home Security offers user-friendly systems with easy-to-navigate interfaces for quick problem identification.
3. Disarm and Reset: If you confirm it’s a false alarm, disarm your system using the control panel or mobile app. Reset the system to clear the alarm and ensure that all components are functioning properly. Regular maintenance and periodic checks can help prevent false alarms in the future.
4. Investigate the Cause: Investigate why the false alarm occurred. Common causes include low batteries, sensor malfunctions, or incorrect installation. Contact Safer Choice Home Security for professional assistance in diagnosing and resolving the issue.
5. Update Emergency Contacts: Ensure your contact information is up-to-date with your monitoring service to avoid unnecessary dispatches by emergency services. Safer Choice Home Security provides comprehensive support to help you manage your system effectively.
Managing false alarms is essential for maintaining the effectiveness of your home security system. Contact Safer Choice Home Security in Laie Hawaii for expert advice and assistance to keep your system running smoothly and securely.

Laie, HI Insights: Population, Influence, and Service Areas

Historically, Lāʻie was a puʻuhonua, a sanctuary for fugitives. While a fugitive was in the puʻuhonua, it was unlawful for that fugitive's pursuers to harm him or her. During wartime, spears with white flags attached were set up at each end of the city of refuge. If warriors attempted to pursue fugitives into the puʻuhonua, they would be killed by sanctuary priests. Fugitives seeking sanctuary in a city of refuge were not forced to permanently live within the confines of its walls. Instead, they were given two choices. In some cases, after a certain length of time (ranging from a couple of weeks to several years), fugitives could enter the service of the priests and assist in the daily affairs of the puʻuhonua. A second option was that after a certain length of time the fugitives would be free to leave and re-enter the world unmolested. Traditional cities of refuge were abolished in 1819.
